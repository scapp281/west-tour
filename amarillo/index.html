Amarillo Introduction

When the town was formally incorporated, the name Amarillo was adopted from a nearby lake. In 1887, Fort Worth and Denver City Railway started laying track in the area. A decade after ranchers began to graze their cattle on the grass-speckled plains. The combination of the railroad and the ranchland led to the establishment of Amarillo's long-standing status as a cattle-shipping capital. To this day, the city "smells like money" most when the Amarillo Livestock Auction is in full swing. Mingle with real cowboys as they go about the business of buying and selling cattle.


Amarillo is the perfect place to get a big taste of Texas even if you only have a small amount of time. From canyons to old Cadillacs and gigantic steaks to grand scenery, the city is an Old West enthusiast's dream. One of the country's best collections of Western art and artifacts can be found at the Panhandle-Plains Historical Museum.


As a destination and a pleasant, inexpensive spot for an overnight stay, Amarillo can be a fun place to spend a weekend, especially for those with a taste for cowboy culture. For those with interests that are more modern, the city boasts a great art museum and beautiful botanical gardens. If you're traveling with kids, don't miss the thrilling rides at Wonderland Park. The whole family will be in awe of the fantastic rock formations at Palo Duro Canyon State Park.
