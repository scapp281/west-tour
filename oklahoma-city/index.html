Oklahoma City Introduction

Oklahoma City was settled on April 1889. Early leaders of the development of the city included Anton Classen, John Shartel, Henry Overholser and James W. Maney. At that time, the area known as the "Unassigned Lands". It was opened for settlement in an event known as "The Land Run". Some 10,000 homesteaders settled in the area that would become the capital of Oklahoma. The population grew doubled between 1890 and 1900. In 1971, Patience Latting was elected Mayor of Oklahoma City, becoming the city's first female mayor. Latting was also the first woman to serve as mayor of a major U.S. city with more than 350,000 residents.


Oklahoma City is the capital of the U.S. state of Oklahoma and its largest city. Oklahoma City has a nickname "The 405", which taken from the area code. Oklahoma City lies along one of the primary travel corridors into Texas and Mexico. The city ranks 27th among United States cities in population. Oklahoma City features one of the largest livestock markets in the world. Oil, natural gas, petroleum products and related industries are the largest sector of the its economy.


A low unemployment rate, continuing steady economic expansion, and a prime Sun Belt location are attractive to new businesses. Its sunny climate, educational and job opportunities, numerous cultural assets, and recreational attractions entice new residents. After experiencing economic difficulties with the 1980s oil slump,struck by nine devastating tornadoes, enduring one of the nation's worst terrorist attacks with the 1995 Murrah Federal Building bombing, Oklahoma City continues a vigorous rebound with a growing population and increasingly diversified economy
